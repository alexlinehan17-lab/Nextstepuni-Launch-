Necessary Knowledge for Leaving Certiﬁcate Exam Success —
Research Dossier
TL;DR
Across every Leaving Certiﬁcate (LC) subject, the dominant cause of mark loss is not lack
of content knowledge but failure to engage with the speciﬁc question asked, ignorance of
marking-scheme conventions (PCLM in English, SRPs in Geography/History,
attempt/blunder/slip in Maths, theory+application+quote in Business, headings/units in
Accounting), and time mismanagement caused by over-writing prepared answers. Chief
Examiner Reports return to the same complaints year after year: rote-learned material,
generic answers, weak interrogation of command words, and incomplete ﬁnal questions.
The single highest-leverage piece of "necessary knowledge" for a DEIS-school student is
the SEC marking scheme grammar — how marks are actually awarded (high/medium/low
partial credit, attempt marks, SRPs, ceilings), because almost every common failure pattern
can be traced back to a student who didn't know the scheme they were being marked
against. Combined with command-word literacy and time-per-mark discipline, this
knowledge raises grades independently of subject content.
The "Necessary Knowledge" tab should be built around interactive simulators that teach
the marking scheme by doing: a command-word decoder, a PCLM/SRP/attempt-mark
mark allocator, a time-per-mark planner with sunk-cost alerts, a sub-task ceiling visualiser
for English, a "spot the trap" examiner-pet-peeve trainer, and sanity-check trainers for
Maths/Science. Static explanation will not move DEIS-school grades; doing-and-getting-
feedback will.
Key Findings
1. The SEC marking scheme has its own grammar — and most students don't know it.
Each subject has a recognisable scoring architecture (PCLM in English, SRPs at 2 marks
each in Geography and most humanities, attempt-mark/blunder(-3)/slip(-1)/misreading(-1)
in Maths, "theory + application + quote" capping in Business ABQ, headings/units/cross-
referenced workings in Accounting, key-phrase matching in Biology). Students who learn
the architecture often outperform students who know more content.
2. Chief Examiner Reports are remarkably repetitive across subjects and decades. The
same complaints recur from 1995 to 2025: rote-learned material, generic answers, failure to
engage with the speciﬁc question, formulaic structure, weak language control, poor time
management, incomplete ﬁnal answers. This consistency is itself the most important
ﬁnding — these are trainable, structural problems, not content gaps.RTÉDublinacademy

A. CROSS-CUTTING STRATEGY AND TECHNIQUE KNOWLEDGE
A1. Command Word Interpretation (the single most-cited issue across reports)
The SEC does not publish a uniﬁed command-word glossary; each subject has its own marking
conventions, but the cross-LC working list is:
State / Name / Identify: 1–2 marks. One word or phrase. No explanation. Most common
error: writing a paragraph when one word is wanted.
Deﬁne: Give the precise syllabus deﬁnition. Examiners hunt for speciﬁc key phrases. In
Chemistry, "isotope" must include "same atomic number but diﬀerent mass numbers"; in
Biology, deﬁnitions are key-phrase-matched.
Outline: Brief points, often bullet-style. Set out main characteristics. Typically 2–4 points;
not full-essay treatment.
Describe: Give an account in your own words. More depth than outline. In sciences, name
the key features and stages.
Explain: Show how or why. Causal/mechanistic. The most-confused word: students
describe instead of explaining. The "explain vs describe" distinction is one of the most
important in the LC.
Discuss: Examine an issue, including diﬀerent viewpoints. Implies more than one
perspective. Should usually conclude.
Evaluate / Assess: Make a judgement using evidence. In Business, "Evaluate" requires the
student's own opinion; missing the opinion caps marks. Examcraftgroup

Compare: Identify similarities (and usually diﬀerences). Must be integrated, not "all of A

Common command-word failure patterns from CERs:
Treating "Discuss" and "Explain" identically (loses the second perspective).
Treating "Evaluate" as "Describe" (no judgement = capped marks in Business, History,
Politics & Society).
Ignoring the modiﬁer in front of a command word: "signiﬁcant insights", "eﬀective use of
disturbing imagery", "deeply embedded values" — the modiﬁers are where the Purpose
marks live in English.
A2. Mark Allocation — How SEC Marking Schemes Actually Work
The published Maths scheme convention (also widely used in other quantitative subjects):
Scale 5B (0, 2, 5) — partial credit for "any correct, relevant step".
Scale 5C (0, 2, 3, 5) — low partial / high partial credit bands.
Scale 10C (0, 4, 7, 10) — three-band partial credit.
Scale 10D (0, 3, 5, 8, 10) — low / mid / high partial credit (most common 10-mark question).
Maths penalty structure (explicit in every published scheme):
Blunders (B) — mathematical errors / omissions: −3 marks.
Slips (S) — numerical slips: −1 mark.
Misreadings (M) — provided task is not oversimpliﬁed: −1 mark.
Attempt mark — typically 30–40% of the part's marks for any correct, relevant step,
including writing down the correct formula from the Tables and Formulae booklet. "If you
write nothing the examiner cannot award any marks."Irevise

English PCLM (Paper 1 and Paper 2 narrative answers):
P — Clarity of Purpose: 30%. Engagement with the actual task asked.
C — Coherence of Delivery: 30%. Logical structure and sustained argument.
L — Eﬃciency of Language: 30%. Genre-appropriate, controlled language.
M — Mechanics: 10%. Spelling, grammar, punctuation.
CRITICAL CEILING RULE (in every English marking scheme): "Given the primacy of
Clarity of Purpose (P), marks awarded for either Coherence of Delivery (C) or Eﬃciency of
Language Use (L) cannot exceed the marks awarded for Clarity of Purpose." This means an
oﬀ-question essay cannot exceed roughly 30% no matter how well written.
Geography SRP system:
SRP = Signiﬁcant Relevant Point = a single piece of factual information worth typically 2
marks.
30-mark essay = 15 SRPs. Most teachers tell students to aim for 17 to be safe.
A diagram with annotation can score SRPs (label = 1 SRP; annotation explaining the
process at that point = additional SRP).
The Marking Scheme states: "each SRP should emerge from the information put forward
by the candidate, leading to an overall coherent response to the question". Identical
sentences across two students may score diﬀerently — context matters.
80-mark essay (e.g. Option) = ~3 paragraphs of 20 marks (4 marks for aspect + 8 SRPs) plus
20 marks for overall coherence.
Business marking conventions:
SRPs appear here too: long questions are marked in SRPs.
ABQ (Applied Business Question) has the "Theory + Application + Quote" rule. Theory
alone is capped (often at ~50%). The marking scheme requires direct quotation from the
case study text to access full marks. Formula taught widely: State (Theory) + Explain
(Detail) + Link (Quote).

Accounting marking conventions:
Headings and units are mark-bearing. Typical penalty: −1 mark per heading omitted (e.g.
"Proﬁt & Loss Account", "For the year ended 31/12/20XX", "Authorised Capital").
The € sign and % sign are required.
Cross-referenced workings: workings must be visible and labelled (W1, W2…). The
"calculator trap" — calculating an adjustment in your head and writing only the ﬁnal ﬁgure
— forfeits all partial marks if the ﬁnal ﬁgure is wrong.
Time per mark in Accounting = ~0.45 mins/mark (27 seconds).
Biology marking conventions:
Section A short questions: typical pattern is ﬁrst six correct = 3 marks each, last correct =
2 marks (varies by question type), or ﬁrst correct = 8/10 marks, then declining. The
precise pattern is published yearly.
Diagrams typically worth 9 marks (6 for diagram + 3 for labelling).
Key-phrase matching: examiners look for speciﬁc phrases (e.g. function of myelin sheath =
"to insulate" or "to speed up the transmission"). Marking schemes list acceptable phrasings.
Bullet-point format is preferred over prose — examiners can ﬁnd key points faster, and the
question demands "5 key points" type answers.
Spelling errors ignored if intent is clear.
History marking conventions:
Documents-Based Question (DBQ) worth 100 marks (20% of paper). Four question types:
Comprehension (~20–40 HL/OL), Comparison (20), Criticism (20), Contextualisation (40
HL).
Long-question essays: marks for cumulative marks per signiﬁcant relevant point, with
overall marks for coherence.
Research Study Report (RSR) worth 100 marks (20% of paper): Outline Plan (15) +
Evaluation of Sources (25) + Extended Essay (50) + Review of Research Process (10). HL:
1,200–1,500 words; OL: 600–800 words.
Modern Languages (French / German / Spanish):
Oral: 25% (HL) or 20% (OL). Marked on Pronunciation (20%), Vocabulary (20%), Structure
(30%), Communication (30%).
Written paper: opinion pieces marked on Communication and Structure (60% of 100
marks). Listening worth ~20%.Windows

A3. Time Allocation Strategy
General LC time-per-mark conversion (approximate):
English Paper 1 (170 marks, 170 mins): ~1 min/mark → 50 mins composition (100 marks),
50 mins comprehension Q A (50 marks), 50 mins Question B (50 marks), 20 mins
read/plan/check.
English Paper 2 (200 marks, 200 mins): 50 mins single text (60), 70 mins comparative
(70), 45 mins poetry (50), 20 mins for unseen poetry. Comparative is the main reason
students run out of time for poetry.
Maths Paper (300 marks, 150 mins): 0.5 mins/mark.
Geography (400 marks, 170 mins): 1.4 mins/mark — short questions ~20–30 mins for 80
marks; each Part B/C (30 marks) = 15 mins; option essay (80) = 35 mins.
Business (400 marks, 180 mins): ABQ 45 mins (80 marks) + Long Questions 27 mins each
(60 marks).
Accounting (400 marks, 180 mins): 27 sec/mark; Section 1 (120) = 54 mins; Section 2 = 90
mins; Section 3 (80) = 36 mins.
Irish Paper 2 poetry: stick to 15-mark / 9-mark / 6-mark pacing strictly.
Sunk-cost rule: when the time allocation for a question expires, move on, even with a part-
answer. Examiners and study guides repeat: "Prioritise attempting all required questions over
completing just a few in full." A part-answer to an unstarted question scores higher than a
perfected answer to one already attempted.
A4. Question Choice Strategy
Read the entire paper ﬁrst (5–10 mins). Mark intended questions ( ✓ ), backups ( ✓ ?),
avoids (✗).
Watch for hidden traps in chosen questions — e.g. a "discuss" sub-part inside a question
that looked descriptive at ﬁrst glance.
The "study roulette" warning: in 2024 LC History, students who only studied the Treaty
for the DBQ found Belfast WW2 instead. Cross-coverage matters more than single-topic
depth.
Sunk-cost fallacy in question choice: students cling to chosen questions because they've
already started; better to bail at 5 minutes if it's not ﬂowing than at 25 minutes when too

A6. Presentation, Layout, Diagrams, Units
Black or blue pen only; pencil only for diagrams/sketches/graphs.
Anything written outside the answer area may not be seen (papers are scanned).
Single line through errors — never use correction ﬂuid; visible work may still earn marks.
Diagrams: title; label; in many subjects, annotate (write a short sentence explaining the
process) — this is the diﬀerence between "labelling" and "annotating" and is mark-bearing
in Geography and Biology.
Graphs (Physics/Chemistry/Maths):
Fill at least three-quarters of the page.
Label both axes with quantity and units.
Independent variable on x, dependent on y (y = f(x) convention).
Mark each point as a dot inside a small circle.
Draw a best-ﬁt line, not "join the dots", unless explicitly required.
Use a transparent ruler.
Units in scientiﬁc calculations: missing units commonly costs the ﬁnal mark; using wrong
unit (e.g. cm³ instead of m³ in pV=nRT) is one of the most common Physics/Chemistry
errors. Temperature must be in Kelvin in gas-law calculations.
State symbols (s, l, g, aq) required in Chemistry equations like Heat of Formation, or marks

B. SUBJECT-SPECIFIC COMMON FAILURE PATTERNS
B1. English — Higher and Ordinary
Sources: Chief Examiner Reports 2001, 2005, 2008, 2013 (most recent). Plus widespread teacher
commentary.
The 1995 CER observed that "many students even very good ones… can no longer write
grammatically. Their writing is frequently illogical, muddled and at times
incomprehensible. Yet they appear to think they are making sense." This complaint has
persisted.
The 2013 Report: "examiners were impressed by evidence of strong textual knowledge and
also the variety of approaches to the analysis of texts. Weaker responses tended to be
characterised by an over-rehearsed formulaic approach."
Comparative Studies is the section where most marks are lost: students write 9 pages
when 5 are wanted; rely on memorised "modes"; treat texts in sequence rather than
analytically; refer to two texts when three are required.
Composition (100 marks): choosing the wrong genre is the commonest cap on marks;
confusing personal essay with discursive; failing to sustain register; failing to integrate the
prompt's speciﬁc phrasing.
Single Text: summarising the plot when the question asked for analysis is the perennial
problem ("am I summarising?" is the question to ask yourself).
Poetry: "Eavan Boland syndrome" — putting all eggs in one poet's basket. The Chief
Examiner has multiple times noted that "personal response should be central" but
candidates "memorise" instead.
Comprehension Question A: students re-phrase rather than infer. The 2013 report
explicitly says students must "draw inferences… synthesize the material, and question or
critically evaluate it".
Comprehension Question B: students treat it as another composition (too long, too
literary). It is a 50-mark functional task. Genre conventions matter (letter, speech, blog,
podcast script, etc.).
Persistent issue: "challenging the terms of a question, perhaps disagreeing with some part
or the entire premise outlined, is an acceptable way in which to approach an answer" —
students don't realise this and write what they think the examiner wants. The Irish Times

Biology:
Three sections: A (short, 100 marks/25%), B (experiments, 60 marks/15%), C (long, 240

Caveats
Chief Examiner Reports are not annual. They are published "periodically" — for English,
the most recent before this dossier is 2013; for Maths 2015; for History 2017; for languages
2016; for Home Economics 2017; for Geography 2012. Any "recurring complaint" ﬁnding
has to be triangulated with newer secondary commentary (RTÉ Learn, Irish Times exam-
day reaction, teacher blogs).
Marking schemes are working documents. The SEC explicitly states: "While a draft
marking scheme is prepared in advance of the examination, the scheme is not ﬁnalised
until examiners have applied it to candidates' work." This means precise mark allocations
vary year to year; the grammar of the scheme (PCLM, SRPs, attempt/blunder/slip) is stable
but speciﬁc weightings shift. The app should explain principles, not memorise speciﬁc
mark-allocations.
Senior Cycle reform is in progress. A series of subjects are being redeveloped 2025–2029
with 40% project assessment + 60% written exam. English is delayed by a year (Fifth Year
start 2027). Construction Studies becomes Construction Technology in 2026. The
"Necessary Knowledge" tab should be future-ﬂagged to update as new speciﬁcations come
online.
Some quoted ﬁgures from secondary sources may date. The "63% of students get

reform); the current grading (H1–H8) has diﬀerent distributions. Always re-check
