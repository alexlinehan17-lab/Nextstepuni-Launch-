# Business 2015 — Examiner Insights

## Source

- **Report type:** Chief Examiner's Report
- **Year:** 2015
- **Levels:** Higher, Ordinary (no Foundation Level for Business)
- **Original filename:** `CHIEFEXAMINERREPORTBUSINESS.pdf`
- **Renamed to:** `2015-chief-examiner.pdf`
- **Paper structure (HL):** Section 1 — eight short-response questions (compulsory). Section 2 — Applied Business Question (ABQ), 80 marks (20%), compulsory, based on a case study. Section 3 — extended response: Part 1 (choose 1 of 3 questions on Units 1, 6, 7) and Part 2 (choose 3 of 4 questions on Units 2, 4, 5).
- **Paper structure (OL):** Section 1 — short questions (10 of 15). Section 2 — Part 1 (choose 1 of 3) and Part 2 (choose 3 of 5).
- **Note on context:** Cohort migration from Ordinary to Higher Level continues — HL share grew from 68% in 2010 to 72% in 2015. The report repeatedly observes that some HL candidates "did not show the level of preparedness to engage with the examination at this level and would have been more suited to the Ordinary level."

## Common errors by question type

### Higher Level — Section 1 (short-response, 80 marks)

- **Q6 — stages in team development.** "Some candidates confused 'storming' with 'brainstorming'" (p.14). Tells you the recall of named stage models was patchy.
- **Q3(a) financial calculation, Q3(b) applying it.** Most candidates calculated Net Profit Percentage accurately in (a). In (b) they "performed less well... applying the information in business decision-making, indicating that the required computational competency was not matched by the necessary understanding and application needed to use the result in a business context" (p.14).
- **Units of measurement omitted** — "Candidates also frequently omitted the units of measurement, e.g. %, €, which are necessary to give meaning to the answers" (p.14).
- **Q1 "span of control".** Explanation of the term well handled; "identifying a situation where a 'narrow span of control may be appropriate' posed difficulties for some" (p.14). Pattern: defining ≠ applying.
- **Q7 "exchange rate".** Explanation OK; "applying the concept to risks for a business proved challenging for many candidates" (p.14). Same pattern.

### Higher Level — Section 2 (Applied Business Question, 80 marks)

The ABQ is the single most important feature of the HL paper — 20% of total marks, compulsory, and the question candidates most consistently get wrong in approach.

- **The two failure modes** (p.15):
  1. "Some candidates score little or no marks as they write business theory without any reference to the ABQ."
  2. "Other candidates appear to treat the ABQ as a comprehension piece and rewrite chunks of material from it, failing to score any marks as a result."
  - The correct approach: business theory **+ a direct relevant quote/phrase/sentence from the ABQ as the link.**
- **Part (A) — "Do you think Martin Harte displayed enterprising skills/characteristics?"** — "A significant number of candidates did not answer the question posed, which was awarded two marks, and proceeded to outline a list of skills/characteristics" (p.15-16). The 2-mark Yes/No answer was the easiest mark on the paper and many missed it.
- **Skill choice without ABQ link** — "The specific enterprising skills/characteristics chosen had to have relevant links in the ABQ to be considered valid" (p.16). Picking generic enterprise skills without anchoring them to specific ABQ moments scored nothing.
- **Part (B)(i) "define the management activity of planning"** — some candidates "wrote extensive answers on the topic of planning, ignoring the question cue 'define'" (p.16). A minority didn't reference the setting of goals — "core aspect of the planning process."
- **Part (C) — evaluation** — "Some evaluations continue to be very superficial and some candidates do not evaluate at all" (p.17). The higher-order skill of evaluation (judgement + justification) is consistently weak.

### Higher Level — Section 3 (extended response)

- **Q1(A) co-operative/conflict relationships** — "A number of candidates provided excellent definitions of the terms 'co-operative relationship' and 'competitive relationship' but did not answer the question set" (p.16-17). Recall ≠ answering.
- **Q1(A) one side of relationship only** — Examples of how *both* parties might co-operate or experience conflict were required; "in many cases only one side of the relationship was presented" (p.17).
- **Q1(B) "illustrate" requires an example** — "While many candidates defined the legal term 'specific performance' they omitted an example, thereby losing marks. A similar requirement existed in Question 3(B) and Question 7(C)(ii)" (p.17).
- **Q1(C)(ii) "List three grounds…"** — "Some candidates wrote paragraphs to explain the protected grounds rather than just naming the grounds, as was required in the question" (p.17). Question cue "List" means list.
- **Q2(C) discussion — repetition of one point** — "Disproportionate focus on 'reduce, reuse, and recycle' in some answers, hence limiting the marks awarded" (p.17). Discussion questions reward distinct points.
- **Q4(A)(i) bar chart** — "Marks were lost for failing to label the chart accurately, for incorrectly plotted points and for failing to title the chart, which is essential information on any chart" (p.18). "Candidates who used graph paper to present the information in general performed better."
- **Q4(A)(ii) communication for Paul's presentation** — "A significant number of candidates evidently misread the question and outlined how Paul might communicate the data on the bar chart to the board of directors" instead of discussing principles of effective communication in the context (p.18).
- **Q5(A)(i) product life cycle** — "Some candidates did not label the axis or title the chart and mixed up some of the stages" (p.18).
- **Q6(A)(ii) utmost good faith** — "Weaker responses explained the principle of utmost good faith as 'telling the truth' rather than 'disclosing all material facts'. Precision of language is required when explaining various business concepts and principles" (p.19).
- **Q7 marketing mix (worst-performing question)** — Average mark 52%. "A significant number of candidates were awarded only nine marks out of a possible twenty-five marks for identifying the product Kurve Ltd had, its pricing strategy and its method of distribution" (p.19). "Answers such as 'product is what is sold' and 'place is where it is sold' were common in the weaker responses" — paraphrasing without theory.

### What separated higher-grade Higher Level answers

- **Careful attention to question wording AND mark allocation** — "Better quality attempts provided evidence of candidates having given careful attention to the wording of the questions and the mark allocation, and planned the use of their time in formulating their responses" (p.15).
- **Theory + linked ABQ quote** — the canonical winning ABQ structure (p.15-16).
- **Distinct points in discussion questions** — avoiding overlap and variations of one core point (p.17).
- **Structured responses with current examples or up-to-date statistics** (p.17).
- **Connecting recent economic events to specific questions asked** — "The knowledge of recent economic events demonstrated by candidates, and their ability to link that knowledge to the specific questions asked, was evident and is to be commended" (p.17, on Q3(A) MNCs in Ireland).

### Ordinary Level — Section 1 (short questions)

- **Average 80% — strongest section.** Recall-type questions readily accessible to most candidates (p.19).
- **Acronym retention errors** — "Some candidates had difficulty with the word 'retention' in 'DIRT'" (p.20).
- **Extended-response definitions** — "limited liability", "innovative", "proposal form" — "some responses were incomplete or inaccurate and marks were lost accordingly" (p.20).
- **No calculator** — "A minority of candidates didn't have a calculator and had some difficulty working out the answer" (p.20). Bring a calculator.
- **Q3 — "free samples" as advertising rather than sales promotion** (p.20). Promotion-mix terminology.
- **Q12 — "production" recorded as marketing mix element rather than "product"** (p.20). Marketing mix vocabulary.
- **Q15 — functional organisation in a private limited company** "proved challenging for the generality of candidates" (p.20).

### Ordinary Level — Section 2 (extended response)

- **The single biggest OL trap — brevity** — "A common problem at the Ordinary level is that only one valid point is often given when two or three points are required. In some cases the same point is repeated" (p.20).
- **Question cues** — "While question cues such as 'list', 'name', 'state' do not present problems, question cues such as 'outline', 'explain' are often not addressed adequately" (p.20).
- **Q7(B) — advantages of selling online** — "Many candidates concentrated their answers on the advantages of working from home rather than the advantages of online selling" (p.21). Misreading of question.
- **Q6(B) bar chart** — "Not drawn to the proper scale in many cases and the axes and title were omitted" (p.21). Same labelling rule as HL.
- **Q6(E) stock control at Mason Motor Ltd** — "Some candidates did not relate their answer on stock control to the stock of new and used cars at Mason Motor Ltd" (p.21). Stimulus material was the context, not just decoration.

### What separated higher-grade Ordinary Level answers

- **Developing each point with detail.** "Brevity and lack of development of points of information presented a significant barrier to good performance by some candidates at the Ordinary level" (p.20).
- **Reading question cues — adjusting answer length to "outline"/"explain" vs "list"/"name"** (p.20).
- **Anchoring answers to the stimulus material** when the question is set in a context (p.21).
- **Application marks are the easiest** — "Marks for the application of knowledge are often some of the easiest to attain and candidates should aim to avail of such opportunities to score marks" (p.20-21).

## Strategic / structural observations

- **Cohort migration from OL to HL.** 72% HL / 28% OL in 2015 vs 68% / 32% in 2010 (p.23). Some who migrated "would have been more suited to the Ordinary level examination" (p.23). Recommendation: "Choose the level at which you will take the examination very carefully" (p.26).
- **Question popularity is consistent year-on-year.** Q1 (Unit 1 Relationships in Business) is always the most popular Part 1 question; Q2 and Q3 (Domestic / International Environment) consistently the least. Q4 (enterprise/management) most popular in Part 2 at both levels (p.11, p.13).
- **Time management is explicit examiner advice.** "Organise time efficiently when sitting the examination and answer the precise question asked. Complete the required number of questions and all sections within questions" (p.26).
- **The ABQ is 20% of HL marks and the most procedurally distinct part of the paper.** Failing to grasp the theory-plus-link approach has "serious impact" (p.23).
- **Question cues drive mark allocation.** "Candidates often respond incorrectly to question cues, resulting in partial or incorrect responses to questions. This results in the loss of marks" (p.24).
- **Diagrams need labels, titles, scaled axes, accurately plotted points** — universal across HL and OL. "Candidates who used graph paper to present the information in general performed better" (p.18).
- **Brief one-word/one-sentence answers continue to lose marks** on questions that require developed points (p.23).

## Misconceptions

- **"Storming" confused with "brainstorming"** in team-development models (HL Q6 short response).
- **"Telling the truth" given as the definition of utmost good faith** — should be "disclosing all material facts" (HL Q6(A)(ii)).
- **"Production" given as a marketing mix element** instead of "product" (OL Q12).
- **"Free samples" classified as advertising** rather than sales promotion (OL Q3).
- **DIRT — confusion on what "R" stands for** (retention).
- **ABQ Part (A) Yes/No questions** — students skip the 2-mark Yes/No verdict and jump to the list, losing the easiest mark on the paper.
- **Defining ≠ answering** — recurring HL pattern. Strong definition followed by an inadequate answer to the actual question.
- **Q7(B) OL — "advantages of working from home"** instead of "advantages of online selling" — wholesale misreading of the question's subject.
- **ABQ as comprehension piece** — rewriting chunks of the case material instead of applying business theory linked to the case.
