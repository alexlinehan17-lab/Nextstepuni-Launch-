# Mathematics 2015 — Examiner Insights

## Source

- **Report type:** Chief Examiner's Report
- **Year:** 2015
- **Levels:** Higher, Ordinary, Foundation (all three covered)
- **Original filename:** `CHEIFEXAMINERREPORTMATHEMATICS.pdf`
- **Renamed to:** `2015-chief-examiner.pdf`
- **Note on context:** 2015 was the first year all candidates (Junior and Leaving Cert) were assessed on every part of the new "Project Maths" syllabus. The report repeatedly reflects on the cohort migration from Ordinary Level to Higher Level driven by the bonus points scheme — this affected grade distributions at both levels.

## Common errors by question type

### Higher Level — Paper 1

- **Q4 (complex numbers in fraction form)** — lowest mean on the paper at 40%. Most candidates knew the procedure for rationalising complex fractions but "struggled to deal with the fractions involved, and a lot of the work produced here was of very poor quality" (p.22).
- **Q5(a) — equation involving a square root.** Most candidates correctly squared both sides and solved the resulting quadratic, but "most candidates did not test the resulting roots, assuming instead that both solutions would satisfy the original equation, which was not the case" (p.21). Squaring can introduce extraneous roots — testing back is required.
- **Q5(c) — turning point of a function.** "Many candidates did not know that solving f'(x) = 0 would yield the x-coordinates of the turning point of the function. In many instances, candidates sought to solve f''(x) = 0 instead, which illustrated a lack of comprehension of the concept of a turning point and its relationship to the derivative" (p.21).
- **Q8(d) — oil slick reaching land (problem-solving in unfamiliar context).** A challenging question with multiple valid approaches. "Candidates showed little initiative in coming up with a solution" (p.23).
- **Q9(c)(ii) — speed in an unfamiliar context.** Many candidates incorrectly attempted to differentiate the function given. They had "clearly linked the concept of speed with calculus in their minds, and they therefore attempted to differentiate the function given in the question, which was not an appropriate approach in the context" (p.24). Examiners noted this was concentrated in particular school centres — see Quotable lines below.

### Higher Level — Paper 2

- **Q1 (probability)** — highest mean on Paper 2 at 84%. Conceptual understanding of basic probability was strong across the cohort (p.21).
- **Q3(c) — angle between two lines.** Many candidates "introduced, unnecessarily, the formula for the angle between two lines in terms of their slopes. This may have resulted from them latching on to the words 'angles between lines' in the question, without giving due thought to the actual question at hand" (p.25). Most did not consider sketching a diagram first, which would have led to a simpler approach.
- **Q7(a) — Pythagoras / right-angled triangle.** "Many errors resulted from incorrect recognition of the right angle and an inability to successfully interpret the information on lengths provided in the text and diagram" (p.24).
- **Q8(e)(i) and 8(f)(ii) — Bernoulli trials, subscript notation.** "Many of the candidates displayed a poor understanding of the significance of subscripts in mathematical notation. The expression p_(n+1) was, in many instances, treated as p_n + 1, leading to incorrect work" (p.26).
- **Q9 — trigonometric model of daylight hours.** Strong adaptive reasoning shown — many candidates either used the derivative approach OR realised "based on their prior knowledge that the longest day in Galway would occur on 21 June, counted the days to that date, and substituted this into the original function" (p.25). A pleasing example of cohort-wide flexibility.

### What separated higher-grade Higher Level answers

- **Depth across the syllabus, not just procedural fluency.** Top candidates "showed a good depth of understanding of the whole syllabus, along with an ability to be both flexible and accurate in their work, and to bring knowledge and skills from a number of different strands to bear on a given question" (p.27).
- **Ability to express reasoning clearly** in adaptive-reasoning questions — "able to express themselves clearly and coherently."
- **Engagement with unfamiliar territory** — "engage with questions with which they were not likely to have been familiar."
- **Perseverance with messy values** — top candidates persisted "even when, because of errors, the numerical values were not user-friendly" (p.20).

### Ordinary Level — Paper 1

- **Algebra-heavy questions are the consistent weak spot.** "Candidates struggled noticeably with questions that involved any significant amount of algebra" (p.17). Q4 (algebra), Q5 (functions), Q9 (functions/graphs) had the lowest means.
- **Q8(e) (formulating an equation).** "Only a minority of the candidates demonstrated an ability to successfully understand the information presented, create an equation and solve it" (p.24).
- **Q9(b) (problem-solving without scaffolding).** "The lack of scaffolding led to a lack of progress for the majority of candidates. Those who resorted to trial and improvement achieved some limited success" (p.24).

### Ordinary Level — Paper 2

- **Q7 — geometry/enlargements.** Lowest mean at 46%. "Many candidates struggled with the construction of the centre of enlargement and the subsequent determination and application of scale factor" (p.22).
- **Q5(a)(i) — trigonometry on a non-right-angled triangle.** Less able candidates "treated the triangle as right-angled or trying to apply the Cosine Rule" — both wrong approaches when sine rule or basic trig was needed (p.23).
- **Q1(a) — counting principle for a 6-digit password with repetition allowed.** "Most candidates were not able to deal with the possible repetition of digits or the fact that the password consisted of six digits. Many students simply worked with a factorial (6!), while others worked with ⁿPᵣ, neither of which displays any appreciable level of understanding" (p.21).

### What separated higher-grade Ordinary Level answers

- **Procedural fluency in a clean order.** Strong candidates "presented the relevant formula, substituted correctly, did the calculation, and gave the answer correct to two decimal places with the relevant unit in both cases, as required" (p.23).
- **Drawing conclusions, not just calculations.** Some candidates wrote the equation, substituted, calculated — and then forgot to write the conclusion. This loses marks (p.22).
- **Perseverance.** The single biggest behavioural difference between OL and HL cohorts — see Quotable line #2.

### Foundation Level

- **Q4 — enlargements.** Lowest mean at 38%. The same difficulty as at OL.
- **Q10(a)(i) vs (a)(ii) — surface area vs volume of two boxes.** Volumes calculated successfully and a correct conclusion drawn; surface areas of the same shapes were much weaker (p.22).
- **Q1(c) — converting profit to a percentage profit.** Calculation of profit was well managed but "the conversion of this to a percentage profit proved difficult for many candidates" (p.23).
- **Q2(a) — tax credit.** Application of the tax credit "was not handled well. Frequently, it was subtracted from the total income at the start instead of from the gross tax due, (i.e., treated like the tax-free allowances of old)" (p.23).

## Strategic / structural observations

- **No choice on any examination paper.** Full syllabus coverage is essential — students cannot rely on dropping topics (p.29).
- **Cohort migration is reshaping grade distributions.** Many candidates who would previously have done OL are now doing HL: "the numbers achieving a grade C or below has shown a marked increase" at HL; "the proportion of candidates achieving a grade B or above has declined significantly" at OL (p.27). When the migrators get C or D at HL, "they have clearly benefitted from being exposed to a higher level of mathematical reasoning and problem solving"; when they get E or below, the choice is harder to defend (p.27).
- **Use of supporting material from past papers should be balanced.** "Examination papers should not be relied on excessively during any extended period of learning, as this might unnecessarily restrict the range of student learning and limit candidates' ability to deal with questions set in unfamiliar contexts" (p.29).
- **Within-paper time management.** Use all of the time. If finished early, review work, checking as many answers as possible (p.32).
- **Show all work, always.** "In some questions, full marks will not be awarded unless candidates show supporting work. Furthermore, marks are generally not awarded for an incorrect answer without any supporting work, whereas if candidates show a procedure which would lead to a solution, then they may get credit for this" (p.31).
- **Round to the required level of accuracy and include units.** "These are skills that are not conceptually challenging, and they should be developed to a high standard through regular practice" (p.30).
- **When you make a mistake, restart cleanly.** "Draw a single line through the incorrect work. Do not use corrector fluid or otherwise make the work illegible — if there is valid work presented and still visible, it may be awarded some marks" (p.32).
- **Don't force a given answer.** "Candidates should avoid trying to force the correct answer when this is given in the question. They should instead state their conclusions based on their own work" (p.32).

## Misconceptions

- **"Always differentiate when you see any question about speed"** — recipe-following without understanding context. Concentrated in some school centres but not others (p.24).
- **f'(x) = 0 vs f''(x) = 0 for turning points** — many candidates set the *second* derivative to zero when finding turning point coordinates (p.21).
- **Squaring both sides without checking** — squaring an equation can introduce extraneous roots; most candidates didn't test (p.21).
- **Subscript notation: p_(n+1) ≠ p_n + 1** — widespread misreading at HL (p.26).
- **Tax credit treated like a tax-free allowance** — subtracted from total income at the start, rather than from the gross tax due (p.23).
- **Counting problems answered with rote 6! or ⁿPᵣ** — without considering whether order matters or whether repetition is allowed (p.21).
- **Compartmentalised syllabus knowledge** — "candidates had more difficulty with questions which required them to draw on multiple strands of the syllabus at once" (p.28). Mathematics is not a "list of discrete rules and definitions to be learned but rather a series of interconnected principles" (p.30).

## Quotable lines

1. **On rote-following recipes (p.24):**
   > *"Some examiners reported that this practice was more likely to occur in centre batches than individually; that is, candidates from some centres clearly had the competence to complete the problem correctly while, in other centres, all candidates immediately attempted to find a derivative. This suggests that candidates in these latter centres, rather than considering and seeking to understand the question presented, were following a direction such as 'always differentiate when you see any question about speed.' This raises questions about the approach to developing strategic competence in the schools or classes concerned."*

2. **On perseverance — the OL/HL difference (p.20):**
   > *"At Higher level in both papers it was apparent that candidates made a determined effort to complete the entire examination paper. They were prepared to make a number of attempts in many questions and to persevere in solving problems even when, because of errors, the numerical values were not user-friendly. However, at Ordinary level, there was little evidence of the same diligence and perseverance when problems arose. Candidates at this level generally abandoned the work as soon as difficulty was encountered, rather than trying different ideas."*

3. **On showing supporting work (p.31):**
   > *"Marks are generally not awarded for an incorrect answer without any supporting work, whereas if candidates show a procedure which would lead to a solution, then they may get credit for this."*

4. **On unfamiliar territory and credit for attempts (p.32):**
   > *"Candidates should be prepared for the unexpected. The syllabus states that they should be able to solve problems in familiar and unfamiliar contexts, so the examination paper would not be fit for purpose if the candidates recognised everything on it. Candidates should expect that there will be questions on the examination paper that, at first glance, they will not know how to complete. They should stay calm, and use the problem-solving skills that they have developed throughout their studies. The less familiar the territory, the more credit is likely to be awarded for attempts at applying appropriate strategies."*
